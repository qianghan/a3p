01

Why This Segment, Why Now

AgentBook has three personas today — Maya, Alex, Jordan — all of whom already run a business with real revenue. Students are a different shape of user: little or no self-employment income, a first-ever tax filing, financial dependence that's only partly resolved, and a fear of tax season that has nothing to do with complexity of their finances and everything to do with never having done it before. None of AgentBook's messaging, onboarding copy, or default categories speak to that user today.

The strategic case isn't the revenue from this cohort in year one — it's small, most students pay $0–$9/month for anything. It's acquisition cost and lifetime value. A student who starts logging a campus job and a tutoring side-hustle in AgentBook at 19 is the exact same person who becomes Jordan (side-hustle + W-2) at 23 and Alex (agency) at 28. Every incumbent below in §2 treats the student as a once-a-year tax-season transaction. AgentBook can instead be the first financial habit a young adult ever forms — which is a much higher-leverage acquisition play than competing on tax-prep price alone.

Mechanics worth building into onboarding directly

Scholarships are fully tax-exempt for full-time Canadian students; RESP capital withdrawals are non-taxable, while EAP (growth/grant) withdrawals are taxable to the student but usually net to zero against the basic personal amount (~$16,452). This is exactly the kind of "sounds scary, is actually fine" fact that belongs in the scholarship-taxability wizard in §5.

Mechanics: F-1/J-1 students are exempt individuals under the Substantial Presence Test for their first 5 calendar years and are FICA-exempt in that window; standard TurboTax/H&R Block don't support Form 1040-NR at all, which is why the university-licensed tools own this niche outright. Treaties matter specifically: the China–US treaty (Article 20) exempts scholarships and limited wages; the India–US treaty (Article 21) is unusual in letting Indian nonresident students claim the standard deduction, which every other nonresident treaty nationality cannot do.

Equity gap worth naming in marketing

International students are barred from every free domestic tool above (TurboTax Free, Cash App Taxes, FreeTaxUSA's free federal tier) purely because Form 1040-NR isn't 1040 — they must pay Sprintax or GLACIER while their domestic classmates file free. That's a real, sympathetic inequity AgentBook can name honestly without overclaiming what it can do about the filing itself (see §6).

Financial-literacy platforms and university programs — the whitespace

Three sponsor-funded gamified/K-12 financial-literacy platforms exist — Banzai (bootstrapped, Provo UT, ~900 bank/credit-union sponsors, 5M+ students reached, all 50 states), EVERFI (46M+ learners historically, now independently owned after Blackbaud sold it in Dec 2024), and Zogo (gamified, bank-sponsored, ~700K+ users mostly under 24, acquired by Peak6 in 2021). All three are extensive at the K-12 level and all have effectively vacated the college-student market — EVERFI's only college-adjacent product targets high schoolers preparing for college costs, not enrolled undergrads, and Banzai's higher-ed offering is a thin resource collection, not a product. Meanwhile individual universities run their own in-house programs — Ohio State's peer-coaching "Scarlet & Gray Financial," UCLA's Financial Wellness Program (which itself licenses EVERFI modules), Michigan's credit-bearing "Financially Savvy Student" course, McGill's "Frugal Scholar" — all staffed by volunteer peer coaches or professional counselors, none of which have a digital, always-on product behind them.

Net read

Nobody has combined (a) a genuinely free/near-free filing-adjacent product, (b) an education-credit and gig-income-aware wizard built specifically for students, and (c) a year-round spend-tracking companion that graduates the user into a full accounting product. Wealthsimple Tax and H&R Block Canada own Canadian sentiment on the filing side; FreeTaxUSA and Cash App Taxes quietly handle the hardest US cases for free but have no student brand; Sprintax/GLACIER own the nonresident-alien mechanics; YNAB owns the "free real product for students" acquisition playbook in budgeting; and Banzai/EVERFI/university coaching programs own pre-college financial literacy but have abandoned the enrolled-college-student moment entirely. That last gap — a digital, always-on financial companion for currently-enrolled students, sitting between the K-12 literacy platforms and the post-grad accounting tools — is exactly the seam AgentBook can occupy, and campus financial-aid/international-student offices and peer-coaching centers are a plausible distribution partner rather than a competitor for it, the same way Sprintax and GLACIER got their university footholds.

Stats worth carrying into messaging directly: Gen Z has the lowest financial literacy rate of any generation, 38% on the TIAA Institute–GFLEC P-Fin Index (2025/2026 wave) versus 47% nationally; 76% of teens report money-related anxiety (Greenlight, 2024); 42% of Gen Z live paycheck-to-paycheck and 63% of community-college students do (Bank of America 2026 / Center for Community College Student Engagement); and in Trellis's Fall 2024 survey of 53,000+ undergrads, 62% worry about running out of money before month's end and 42% have less than $100 in savings. These support the fear-reduction and companion pillars in §7 with real numbers rather than an assumed pain point.

03

Role Decision — No New Role Needed

Recommendation: do not introduce a "student" role. Keep the student as an ordinary individual account and add "student" as a value of the existing AbTenantConfig.businessType field.

That field already exists (businessType String @default("freelancer")) and already drives Maya/Alex/Jordan-style persona behavior without any concept of a formal role or permission tier. A role, in this codebase, is an auth/authorization primitive (public.Role) that gates what a user is allowed to do. A student doesn't need different permissions from a freelancer — they need different defaults, copy, and emphasis. That's exactly what a businessType value is for.

What changes per businessType = "student"What does NOT change
Onboarding wizard steps and copy (§5)Auth model, session handling, public.Role/Team tables
Default expense categories (tuition, textbooks, campus meal plan, rent-split, part-time job, tutoring/gig income, scholarship/grant)Ledger engine, journal entry validation, double-entry constraints

Which tax skills are surfaced first (education credits, dependent-status check, scholarship taxability, treaty lookup for international students)Number of plugins (still 4) or skills (still additive, no forking of existing skills)

This mirrors the same pattern already validated by the in-flight Startup plugin design: a persona is a configuration and content concern, not a schema concern. It keeps the change additive and low-risk — a new businessType enum value, a handful of persona-conditional branches in onboarding/prompt-building code, and no new tables, roles, or permission checks anywhere in the stack.

05

Onboarding Redesign

The current 7-step wizard (jurisdiction, currency, fiscal year, chart of accounts, ...) is built for someone who already thinks of themselves as running a business. Students don't, and jargon at step one is where they bounce. The student path should collapse to four screens, with the existing wizard fully preserved for the freelancer/business path.

SCREEN 1
Are you someone's dependent?

Plain-language yes/no/not-sure, with a one-line explainer. This single answer reorders which tax skills get surfaced first and whether a "share a summary with a parent" prompt appears later.

SCREEN 2
Where do you go to school, and where's home?

Sets jurisdiction + region automatically. A "studying on a visa?" toggle here routes straight into the international track (§6) instead of a generic country picker.

SCREEN 3
Where does your money come from?

A checklist, not a chart of accounts: campus job, tutoring/gig apps, scholarship or grant, RESP/529 withdrawal, allowance/family support, freelance/side hustle. Each checked box silently seeds the right default categories and skill priorities.

SCREEN 4
Connect a bank or start manually

Same Plaid flow as every other persona — but framed as "see your spending instantly" rather than "reconcile your books," and with an explicit skip-for-now path since many students share a parent's account initially.

Onboarding features that make it attractive, not just short

  • .edu / student-status verification unlocks the free tier instantly (same pattern as GitHub Student Pack, Spotify, Amazon Prime Student) — no credit card, no "trial that becomes a charge" anxiety, which is the single biggest trust-killer for a first-time signer-up in this age group.
  • "First return ever" explainer mode — inline tooltips on every unfamiliar term (W-2, 1098-T, dependent, standard deduction) rather than a link out to a glossary. This is the direct counter to the fear-of-doing-it-wrong that §2's research shows no incumbent addresses in-product.
  • Scholarship/grant taxability wizard — a short decision tree (tuition/fees/required books = generally tax-free; room/board/stipends = generally taxable) surfaced the moment a student checks "scholarship or grant" in Screen 3. No mainstream product makes this a guided flow; it's usually a paragraph of IRS/CRA prose.
  • Gig-income auto-categorization from day one — reuses the existing Plaid sync + AI categorization engine so a DoorDash or Wyzant deposit is recognized and pre-labeled before the student ever manually enters an expense category.
  • Optional read-only parent/guardian share link — reuses the existing CPA-portal share pattern (a scoped, read-only report link), not a new access-control role. Builds trust for a segment that is often still financially entangled with parents without making "student" a second-class or supervised account.
  • Mobile-first from minute one — the PWA install banner, offline-capable receipt capture, and background sync AgentBook shipped in the last few weeks (#194–#196) are a real differentiator for a mobile-native audience; the onboarding flow should surface the install prompt earlier for this persona than it does today.
  • Telegram-first daily habit — the existing Telegram-first architecture is arguably the single best fit for students of any AgentBook persona: no app to install, receipt photos from a phone that's already in hand, and the exact zero-friction channel every incumbent above lacks between tax seasons.

International Students

International students are a distinct compliance regime, not just a locale variant, and the product needs to say so explicitly rather than funnel them through the domestic wizard.

DifferenceImplication for AgentBook
US: nonresident alien status for F-1/J-1 holders in their first 5 calendar years — files Form 1040NR, not 1040; generally can't claim the standard deduction (narrow India-treaty exception); is FICA-exempt on on-campus/CPT/OPT wages.AgentBook should not attempt to be the 1040NR filing engine — that's Sprintax/GLACIER's job and universities already pay for those seats. AgentBook's honest lane is everyday spend tracking, deadline reminders, and a plain-English "what does nonresident alien even mean for me" explainer, with an outbound hand-off to the student's university-licensed Sprintax/GLACIER seat when it's time to actually file.
Tax treaties — many home countries have a US or Canada treaty exempting scholarship income or wages up to a threshold; income is often reported on Form 1042-S instead of (or alongside) a W-2.A treaty-lookup skill keyed on home country + visa type, and 1098-T/1042-S-aware document scanning rather than assuming every income document is a W-2.
Visa-restricted work — most F-1/J-1 students cannot legally take on arbitrary gig-platform or freelance income the way the domestic "side hustle" story assumes; only specific authorized categories (on-campus, CPT, OPT) are allowed.The gig-income messaging and default categories from §5 must branch for the international track — don't market "track your DoorDash side hustle" to a visa holder for whom that could be a status violation. This is a compliance-sensitive copy difference, not just a feature toggle.
No SSN/SIN at signup — many international students haven't been issued one yet, or use an ITIN instead.Onboarding must accept "I don't have this yet" as a valid, complete answer rather than blocking on it — most identity-gated flows elsewhere in AgentBook assume a completed jurisdiction profile.
Canada — international students on a study permit may still be required or entitled to file (e.g. to claim the GST/HST credit) even with zero income, and residency-for-tax-purposes is determined by residential ties, not visa status.A "you might be owed money even with $0 income" nudge is a strong, honest acquisition hook that costs nothing to build (it's a rules check, not a filing) and that no Canadian incumbent foregrounds for this segment.
Positioning decision

Don't compete with Sprintax on 1040NR mechanics — the volume per user is low, the regulatory risk of getting a nonresident return wrong is high, and universities already provide it for free. Compete on everything Sprintax doesn't do: daily spend tracking, remittance/currency awareness for money sent home or received from family abroad, deadline reminders, and translating "what does my 1042-S mean" into plain language. Partner-link to Sprintax/GLACIER for the filing step itself.

07

Marketing & Message Kit

Master narrative: "Your first W-2 shouldn't be scarier than your first exam." Two value pillars, both needed — tax filing is the acquisition hook, the finance companion is the retention story.

Pillar 1 — Benefits of filing (correctly)

Money left on the table
"H&R Block's free tier covers your 1098-T. TurboTax's doesn't — and won't tell you that until you're halfway through."

A direct, factual comparison ad (per §2 research) aimed at the exact moment a student hits an unexpected upgrade wall on a competitor.

Fear reduction
"You don't need to understand tax law. You need someone to read your W-2 to you."

Directly targets first-time-filer anxiety — the single most-cited pain point for this segment and the one no incumbent's UI addresses head-on.

Dependent-status stakes
"Filing wrong doesn't just cost you money — it can cost your parents' return too."

Surfaces the dependent/independent interaction early, which is both genuinely useful and a natural parent-facing referral moment.

Free money, zero income
"Even if you made $0 this year, you might be owed a GST/HST credit." Canada

An honest, low-effort hook unique to the Canadian international-student research finding in §6.

Pillar 2 — AgentBook as a year-round companion

Habit, not scramble
"Text us a photo of the receipt now. Thank yourself in April."

Leans on the Telegram-first architecture and the PWA capabilities shipped in #194–#196 to sell a daily habit, not a seasonal chore.

Grows with you
"The same app that tracks your tutoring gigs at 20 files your freelance business at 25."

Makes the Jordan/Alex growth path explicit and visible to the student, turning today's low-ARPU signup into tomorrow's full-price customer.

Campus channel
"Ask your international student office, financial aid office, or econ club about AgentBook."

Distribution via campus partnerships and student-ambassador referral codes, mirroring how Sprintax and GLACIER got their university footholds.

Copy guardrail

Every "free money" and "we found you a refund" message must be paired with a visible statement that AgentBook drafts guidance, not filed returns, and is not a registered CPA/e-file agent — the same disclosure pattern used for the Startup plugin. Tax-season scam and phishing volume specifically targets students; overclaiming here is both a compliance risk and a trust risk with a skeptical audience.

09

Risks & Compliance Guardrails

  • Not a filing engine. AgentBook drafts guidance and categorizes income; it is not a registered e-file agent or CPA firm, and should route actual submission through IRS Free File/certified partners (US) or NETFILE-certified software (Canada) — same posture as the Startup plugin design.
  • Visa-status sensitivity. Never market gig/freelance income features to the international track without the visa-authorization caveat — see §6.
  • Verification abuse. .edu-gated free tier needs a real verification provider (SheerID-style) rather than a simple email-domain regex, to avoid a free-tier abuse vector.
  • Parent-share link scope. The optional read-only summary must be a deliberately scoped, revocable link — not a second login or a shared password — to avoid creating an ad hoc second "role" through the back door of a feature.

PHASE B
Student Free plan + verification
Scoped, needs a decision
  • Smaller than originally scoped. A $0 free BillPlan row already exists and every new signup defaults to it — there is no new billing primitive to build. The open question is purely: does a verified student get anything different from today's generic free tier, and if so, what gates it?
  • Three verification options, increasing cost and anti-abuse strength: (1) none — businessType=student is self-declared, same as freelancer/consultant today; (2) a lightweight in-house check (.edu email domain match at signup, no vendor contract, ships in a day); (3) a paid vendor (SheerID/ID.me) — real anti-fraud, but a contract/budget decision, not an engineering one.
  • If students get an enhanced free tier (e.g. telegram_bot: true, a higher AI-message quota than the generic free plan's 100/mo), that's a new BillPlan row + a gate on the businessType/verification flag — small, but needs the quotas decided first.
PHASE C
Scholarship-taxability & education-credit skill
Scoped, needs content sign-off
  • Mechanically small — follows the existing pattern exactly: add a manifest to BUILT_IN_SKILLS in plugins/agentbook-core/backend/src/server.ts, an inline handler in classifyAndExecuteV1(), seed via POST /agent/seed-skills.
  • The real work is the content: the actual decision-tree rules (US: qualified vs. non-qualified scholarship expenses, AOTC/LLC eligibility and phase-outs, dependent-status restrictions; Canada: T2202 tuition transfer, RESP EAP vs. capital-withdrawal treatment). I can draft this from the research already done in §2, but it's tax guidance a real user will act on — see the sign-off question below.
PHASE D
International / visa track
Scoped, needs a schema decision
  • Unlike Phase A, this genuinely needs a small schema addition — nothing in AbTenantConfig today captures visa status or home country. A migration (e.g. visaStatus, homeCountry fields) is a real, reviewable change, not a config value.
  • Treaty-lookup skill needs a country → treaty-terms data table. I have verified data for a couple of major sending countries (China Art. 20, India Art. 21) from §2's research; a table covering the top ~10 F-1/J-1 source countries is a research task I can do, but again — see the sign-off question.
  • Sprintax/GLACIER hand-off: a static informational link costs nothing and can ship immediately. An actual referral/affiliate partnership is a business-development conversation only you can start.
  • 1042-S-aware document scanning extends the existing Gemini OCR skill — feasible, needs a few sample 1042-S images to tune the extraction prompt against.
PHASE E
Campus distribution
Cheaper than scoped — reuses shipped infra
  • Student-ambassador referral codes need zero new schema. BillReferralCode/BillReferral (shipped in PR #169-171/#180) already give every tenant a unique code, invite tracking, and a reward-months mechanism via GET /api/v1/agentbook-billing/referrals/me. A "student ambassador" is just this same code, distributed through a campus channel instead of word of mouth — no new engineering, a copy/positioning exercise at most.
  • The parent read-only share link needs zero new schema too. AbCpaReviewLink + the /review/[token] route (shipped) already expose a scoped, revocable, expirable read-only P&L/summary link with no CPA-specific coupling in the data it returns. Reusing it for "share a summary with a parent" is a UI entry point + copy change, not a new access-control model.
  • International-student-office / financial-aid-office / peer-coaching-center partnerships are pure business development — outreach, a pitch, a relationship. That part is entirely yours; I can draft outreach copy or a one-pager if useful, but I can't make the calls.
